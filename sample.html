A Tale of Two Cities


About the Sample

Here are the results from the first paragraph of Charles Dicken's 1859 novel "A Tale of Two Cities".

The sample contains the first 337 words from the first chapter. This selection is segmented by whole sentences and sentence fragments. These sentence fragments were subdivided by punctuation and non-word characters such as commas, parenthesis, colons, and semicolons.

Notes

The first ten sentences follow a pattern of repetition. This uniform word count creates a coordinated structure that can introduce counter-intuitive ideas while promoting a theme of duality and conflict. This uniformity was an intentional decision made by Dickens.

After the opening of the paragraph, Dickens begins to expand the word count, increase momentum, and then present the context of the story.

Notes

During the opening phrase, the amount of words per sentence stays consistent, but the amount of syllables per sentence increases. As more context is presented, the reader is slowed down by a higher syllable-density per word. This could be an intentional decision or side-effect as Dickens introduces the context for the story.

Below are the breath units statistics




Notes

The average breath units is 1.39 (syllables per words). The median is 1.35 breath units. If you look at Sentence 9, you can develop a sense of the meter Dickens has presented on the first page.


"... it was the winter of despair..."

Sentence 9, breath unit: 1.33

This sentence is part of the coordinated structure Dickens has built in the opening sentences. As expected, the average and median of the whole selection is close to the first 58 words of the book. However, similar sentence structures can be found later in this selection:


"... strange to relate..."

Sentence Fragment 37, breath unit: 1.33

Despite the rise in word-count and syllable-count, Dickens maintains a consistent ratio of syllables to words.

But what about Sentence Fragment 32? Why is the syllable to word ratio 4.0?


"... supernaturally deficient in originality..."

Sentence Fragment 32, breath units: 4.0

This sentence features several phonetic troughs that cause the reader to slow down.

At initial glance, you might be inclined to believe that Sentence Fragment 29 would have a high breath unit with a word count of 28 and a syllabic count of 45, but the ratio of these two is only 1.61.
